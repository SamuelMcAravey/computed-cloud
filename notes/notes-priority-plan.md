# Notes Priority Plan

This is the short list that rose to the top after reviewing the fact bank, the
published posts, and the resume material.

The main filter was:

- strong evidence in existing source material
- distinct from what is already on the blog
- small enough to work as a note first
- personal enough to sound like you, not like a cleaned-up article

## Top candidates right now

### 1. Recovery paths are part of the product

- Why it rises: this is one of your clearest repeated ideas across payments,
  integrations, support, and reliability work.
- Best note angle: a short first-person note about partial failure and why "an
  engineer can fix it later" is not a product strategy.
- Future expansion: yes, easily.

### 2. If one person has to do the daily math, that's a problem

- Why it rises: this is one of the best bakery-to-systems crossover ideas and it
  feels personal without oversharing.
- Best note angle: being the bottleneck was the real forcing function behind the
  bakery planning system.
- Future expansion: yes, especially as a founder/ops piece.

### 3. Payments is not one thing

- Why it rises: this is specific, credible, and useful to both engineers and
  founders.
- Best note angle: checks, ACH, wires, cards, RTP, and FedNow are not just
  transport choices. They create different support and recovery work.
- Future expansion: yes.

### 4. At some point, the sync stopped being glue code

- Why it rises: the Azure Data Factory -> code move is one of the strongest
  concrete "what changed and why" stories in the material.
- Best note angle: orchestration tools stop being enough once the rules and
  debugging pressure get real.
- Future expansion: yes, probably a full case study.

### 5. Owning the P-and-L changed how I think about software

- Why it rises: this is one of the most distinctive non-technical angles in the
  bakery material.
- Best note angle: once labor, waste, and manual re-entry hit the same sheet,
  weak internal tooling stops feeling theoretical.
- Future expansion: maybe, but it is a strong note either way.

### 6. A single big machine can take you pretty far

- Why it rises: strong opinion, clearly yours, and it fits the "survives
  reality" framing.
- Best note angle: complexity is a staffing and debugging bill, not just an
  architecture choice.
- Future expansion: yes.

### 7. Metrics are useful right up until they become theater

- Why it rises: it fits your leadership material and pushes against common
  management fluff.
- Best note angle: the only metrics worth keeping are the ones that change an
  actual decision.
- Future expansion: maybe.

### 8. Reactive programming stuck with me

- Why it rises: unusual background detail, more personal, and a good way to add
  range to the notes section.
- Best note angle: the tech stack changed, but the mental model still shapes how
  you think about workflows, retries, and state.
- Future expansion: maybe.
