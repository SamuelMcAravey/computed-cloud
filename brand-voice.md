## 2) Brand Voice (Core)

### Attribute 1: Plainspoken
- Definition: Use simple, direct language that a non-technical business owner can understand.
- Sounds like: "We build small-business sites that make it easy to call or request a quote." "You send the basics. We build the first draft."
- Does NOT sound like: "We leverage innovative solutions to optimize digital engagement." "World-class web experiences."

### Attribute 2: Professional
- Definition: Clear, respectful, and businesslike without being stiff.
- Sounds like: "We respond within PLACEHOLDER: response window." "You will always know the next step."
- Does NOT sound like: "No worries, we got you." "We're obsessed with your success."

### Attribute 3: Practical
- Definition: Focus on what the client gets and what happens next, not abstract benefits.
- Sounds like: "Four core pages, one revision round, and launch support." "We help you choose the right pages for your business."
- Does NOT sound like: "Transform your brand presence." "Unlock the power of the web."

### Attribute 4: Direct
- Definition: Short sentences, clear CTAs, and no filler.
- Sounds like: "Start with a short intake form." "Review the preview link and send edits."
- Does NOT sound like: "At the end of the day, the journey begins with a conversation."

### Attribute 5: Calm
- Definition: Confident and steady; reduces stress for busy owners.
- Sounds like: "We keep scope tight so projects stay on time." "We'll tell you what we need and when."
- Does NOT sound like: "Hurry, spots are limited." "Don't miss out."

## 3) Tone Modulation (Contextual)

### Homepage hero + primary CTA
- Emotional intent: Reassuring, confident, and clear about the next step.
- Emphasize: Simple scope, clear outcome, and how to start.
- Avoid: Big promises, urgency pressure, or vague benefits.

### Services descriptions
- Emotional intent: Practical and matter-of-fact.
- Emphasize: What's included, what's optional, and what is not included.
- Avoid: Overpromising results or using undefined terms.

### About page / story
- Emotional intent: Human and credible, without self-praise.
- Emphasize: Why you do this work and how you approach projects.
- Avoid: Long backstory, hype, or claims.

### Contact page and forms
- Emotional intent: Helpful and low-friction.
- Emphasize: What information is needed and why.
- Avoid: Long instructions, legalese, or pressure.

### Errors, empty states, and system messages
- Emotional intent: Calm and solution-oriented.
- Emphasize: What happened and how to fix it.
- Avoid: Blame, jargon, or humor.

## 4) Writing Rules (Hard Constraints)
- Sentence length: 8-20 words; avoid sentences over 24 words.
- Paragraph length: 1-3 sentences; keep blocks short.
- Voice: Active voice by default; use passive only when the actor is unknown.
- Reading level: Grade 6-9.
- Jargon policy: Avoid technical terms unless defined in the same sentence.
- Contractions: Allowed; use sparingly for a natural tone.
- Capitalization: Sentence case for headings and UI labels; avoid Title Case in body copy.
- Punctuation: Prefer periods; limit exclamation points to rare, intentional use.
- Numbers, dates, currency: Use numerals for numbers 10+; use "$" with no trailing zeros (e.g., $995). Use plain dates (e.g., "March 10").
- Acronyms and abbreviations: Spell out on first use; avoid unless common to small-business owners.

## 5) Messaging Pillars

### Pillar 1: Clear scope
- Meaning in practice: Define what's included and what's optional.
- Approved themes and ideas: core pages, timeline steps, revision limits, launch checklist.
- Disallowed language or framing: "Unlimited," "everything included," or open-ended promises.

### Pillar 2: Simple process
- Meaning in practice: Show the steps and reduce uncertainty.
- Approved themes and ideas: intake, build, review, launch; what the client provides.
- Disallowed language or framing: Vague "collaboration" language without specifics.

### Pillar 3: Useful outcomes
- Meaning in practice: Connect the site to customer actions without promises.
- Approved themes and ideas: clear calls-to-action, easy contact, clean structure.
- Disallowed language or framing: Guaranteed leads, rankings, or revenue.

### Pillar 4: Client control
- Meaning in practice: Emphasize ownership and transparency.
- Approved themes and ideas: client-owned domain, access to content, clear handoff.
- Disallowed language or framing: Lock-in, exclusivity, or platform dependence.

## 6) Audience Definition

### Persona 1: Local owner-operator
- Role / situation: Runs a small business and wears many hats.
- What they care about most: A site that looks professional and is easy to update.
- What frustrates them: Long timelines, unclear scope, or tech jargon.
- What builds trust: Clear steps, direct answers, transparent pricing.
- What erodes trust: Vague promises, hidden costs, or pressure tactics.

### Persona 2: Small business manager
- Role / situation: Manages marketing or operations for a small team.
- What they care about most: Predictable process and clear approval points.
- What frustrates them: Rework due to unclear requirements or missing assets.
- What builds trust: A checklist, timeline, and easy feedback loop.
- What erodes trust: Surprise changes or unclear responsibilities.

### Persona 3: New business founder
- Role / situation: Starting a business and needs a credible web presence fast.
- What they care about most: Guidance, speed, and clarity on what matters.
- What frustrates them: Too many options or unclear priorities.
- What builds trust: Simple recommendations and a clear minimum viable site.
- What erodes trust: Overly complex packages or aggressive upsells.

## 7) Vocabulary & Language Control

### Preferred language
- "simple"
- "clear"
- "direct"
- "small-business"
- "local"
- "scope"
- "timeline"
- "preview"
- "launch"
- "request a quote"
- "contact"

### Avoid entirely
- "best-in-class"
- "world-class"
- "award-winning"
- "innovative"
- "disruptive"
- "synergy"
- "cutting-edge"
- "optimize"
- "scale" (unless defined)
- "guaranteed"

### Allowed with care
- "SEO" (define: basic search setup, titles, descriptions)
- "conversion" (define: calls or quote requests)
- "strategy" (define: page order or messaging priorities)
- "premium" (only if defined by scope or materials)

## 8) CTA System

### Learn / explore
- Approved patterns: "See what's included" "View services" "Learn about the process"

### Contact / conversation
- Approved patterns: "Request a quote" "Send a message" "Schedule a call" "Contact us"

### Start / onboarding
- Approved patterns: "Start the intake" "Begin your project" "Get started"

### CTA tone rules
- Use verbs that describe the next action.
- Keep CTAs 2-4 words when possible.
- Avoid urgency language unless time-bound and true.

### CTA anti-patterns
- "Claim your spot"
- "Book now" (unless scheduling is live)
- "Get instant results"
- "Join the revolution"

## 9) Microcopy Standards

### Form labels and helper text
- Use plain labels: "First name," "Email," "Business name."
- Helper text should explain why the info is needed.
- Example: "Phone (optional)" or "Used only to schedule a quick call."

### Validation errors
- Be direct and specific.
- Example: "Please enter a valid email address."

### Success states
- Confirm receipt and set expectations.
- Example: "Thanks - we received your message. We'll reply within PLACEHOLDER: response window."

### Empty states
- Explain what should appear and how to proceed.
- Example: "No projects listed yet. PLACEHOLDER: next step."

### Confirmation dialogs
- State the action and the consequence.
- Example: "Submit this request? You can edit details later."

## 11) Guardrails & Compliance

### Forbidden claims and phrases
- "Best" or "#1"
- "Guaranteed results"
- "Proven system"
- "Trusted by PLACEHOLDER: number"
- "Certified"

### Risky areas
- Security claims
- SEO rankings or lead guarantees
- Pricing commitments outside of written agreements
- Compliance claims (ADA, HIPAA, etc.)

### Safe alternative phrasing patterns
- "We focus on..."
- "We keep scope to..."
- "We provide..."
- "We can discuss..."
